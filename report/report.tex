\section{Introduction}
Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for training autonomous agents in complex decision-making environments. Unlike supervised learning, RL agents learn through direct interaction --- receiving rewards for desirable actions and penalties for mistakes.

In this project, we apply \textbf{Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)}---a state-of-the-art actor-critic algorithm---to \textbf{autonomous highway driving}. The agent must navigate through multi-lane traffic, avoid collisions, and make intelligent lane-change decisions to overtake slower vehicles using the \texttt{highway-fast-v0} environment. To demonstrate a foundational understanding of Reinforcement Learning mathematics, both the PPO and \textbf{Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C)} algorithms were implemented \textbf{completely from scratch using PyTorch}, specifically avoiding high-level ready-made libraries. This allowed for a direct comparison of PPO's clipping mechanism against the standard A2C baseline.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Train an RL agent to: (1) Maximize speed for progress, (2) Avoid all collisions, (3) Execute safe overtakes, and (4) Maintain stable driving behavior.

\subsection{Environment: Highway-fast-v0}
The environment simulates a 5-lane highway with multi-agent interaction and realistic physics.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Actor-Critic Architecture}
We use an actor-critic framework with shared hidden layers (256x256 MLP). The \textbf{Actor ($\pi_\theta$)} decides the action, while the \textbf{Critic ($V_\phi$)} evaluates the state. 
\begin{equation}
    \text{Input}(s) \rightarrow \text{FC}(256) \rightarrow \text{FC}(256) \rightarrow [ \pi_\theta(a|s), V_\phi(s) ]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) \& PPO}
We use GAE ($\gamma=0.99, \lambda=0.95$) to balance bias and variance. PPO prevents destructive policy updates using a \textbf{clipped surrogate objective}:
\begin{equation}
    L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min\left( r_t\hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(r_t, 0.8, 1.2)\hat{A}_t \right) \right]
\end{equation}
The total loss $L = L^{\text{CLIP}} - 0.5 L^{\text{VF}} + 0.01 H[\pi_\theta]$ includes value loss and entropy bonus for exploration. Building these custom PyTorch engines allowed direct insight into the differences between PPO's clipped surrogate objective and A2C's standard policy gradient, providing a robust modular framework for autonomous driving.

\section{Implementation \& Detailed Analysis}
Training was executed on an NVIDIA RTX 3050. The custom PyTorch architectures (PPO and A2C) were trained from scratch for 50,000 timesteps using optimized learning rates ($3\text{e-}4$ for PPO and $7\text{e-}4$ for A2C). We conducted an empirical analysis comparing their convergence, stability, and final evaluation performance.

\subsection{Results and Detailed Analysis}
The generated analytics provide a definitive comparison between PPO and the baseline A2C framework.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Convergence and Learning Efficiency (Figure 1):} The learning curve clearly shows PPO converging significantly faster and reaching a much higher overall average reward compared to A2C. PPO quickly surpassed an average reward of 35 over the 100-episode rolling window, while A2C struggled to cross 30, demonstrating PPO's superior sample efficiency. 
    \item \textbf{Survival and Safety (Figure 2):} In the \texttt{highway-fast-v0} environment, greater survival steps directly correlate to safer driving behavior. Figure 2 proves that the custom PPO agent consistently maintained average episode lengths above 45-50 steps within its rolling window, meaning it learned to safely navigate and overtake traffic without destructive collisions. A2C, meanwhile, crashed more frequently, showing high variance in its survival trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Final Policy Stability (Figure 3):} Evaluating the final 100 episodes directly isolates the converged policy's performance. The reward distribution histogram highlights a massive clustering of PPO episodes in the highest reward bins. In contrast, A2C's episodes are spread widely across lower-return bins, indicating that standard policy gradients without clipping suffer from unrecoverable drops in policy quality even late in training.
    \item \textbf{Consistency Profiling (Figure 4):} The final survival bar chart emphasizes not just the higher mean survival of PPO, but crucially its tighter standard deviation. PPO's clipped objective function prevented the agent from suddenly forgetting its driving policy, leading to highly consistent, safe lane-changing maneuvers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
Building both algorithms physically from scratch using PyTorch confirmed the immense mathematical advantage of Proximal Policy Optimization. By limiting the magnitude of policy updates via its clipped surrogate objective, PPO completely outperformed standard A2C in stability, reward maximization, and overall collision avoidance in a high-density traffic simulation.
