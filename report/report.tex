\section{Introduction}
Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful and scalable paradigm for training autonomous agents in highly complex, stochastic, and dynamic decision-making environments. Unlike supervised learning, which relies on a pre-labeled dataset of correct actions, RL agents learn entirely through direct interaction with their environment. They receive scalar rewards for desirable actions and penalties for mistakes, continually updating their policy to maximize the expected cumulative return over time. 

In this project, we explore the application of discrete-action deep reinforcement learning algorithms to the problem of \textbf{autonomous highway driving}. Driving on a multi-lane highway requires a delicate balance of multiple competing objectives. The vehicle must navigate through dense and unpredictable traffic, avoid catastrophic collisions with both static and dynamic obstacles, maintain a high forward velocity to make meaningful progress, and make intelligent lane-change decisions to overtake slower vehicles safely.

To tackle this challenge, we implement and rigorously evaluate \textbf{Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)}---a state-of-the-art actor-critic algorithm that has become the default RL algorithm in the industry due to its ease of tuning and robust performance. To establish a strong baseline and demonstrate a foundational understanding of the underlying mathematics of policy gradients, we also implemented the \textbf{Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C)} algorithm. Both algorithms were developed \textbf{completely from scratch using PyTorch}, deliberately avoiding the use of high-level pre-packaged RL libraries like Stable-Baselines3 or RLlib. This bare-metal implementation approach allowed for an unprecedented level of control and a direct, unbiased comparison of PPO's clipped surrogate objective mechanism against the standard A2C baseline. 

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The objective is to train an RL agent to safely and efficiently operate a vehicle in a simulated multi-lane highway environment. The agent must simultaneously satisfy four primary directives:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Maximize Speed for Progress:} Travel as far down the highway as possible within the allocated time steps.
    \item \textbf{Avoid All Collisions:} Ensure exactly zero collisions with other vehicles, as collisions are terminal and highly penalized.
    \item \textbf{Execute Safe Overtakes:} Identify slower vehicles ahead and execute safe lane changes to bypass them.
    \item \textbf{Maintain Stable Driving Behavior:} Minimize unnecessary lane changes and steering oscillations to mimic comfortable human driving.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Markov Decision Processes}
We model the autonomous driving task as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), defined by the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{P}, \mathcal{R}, \gamma)$. At each discrete time step $t$, the agent observes a state $s_t \in \mathcal{S}$, selects an action $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$ according to its policy $\pi(a_t | s_t)$, and transitions to a new state $s_{t+1}$ governed by the environment's transition dynamics $\mathcal{P}(s_{t+1}|s_t, a_t)$. The agent receives a scalar reward $r_t = \mathcal{R}(s_t, a_t)$. The goal is to find an optimal policy $\pi^*$ that maximizes the expected discounted return:
\begin{equation}
J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r_t \right]
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the discount factor.

\subsection{Policy Gradients and Actor-Critic}
Policy gradient methods directly parameterize the policy $\pi_\theta(a|s)$ with a neural network and update its parameters $\theta$ by performing gradient ascent on the objective $J(\pi_\theta)$. The standard policy gradient estimator is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\theta J(\pi_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) \hat{A}_t \right]
\end{equation}
where $\hat{A}_t$ is an estimator of the advantage function, which measures how much better an action $a_t$ is compared to the average action at state $s_t$. Actor-Critic methods utilize a separate neural network, the Critic $V_\phi(s)$, to estimate the value function, reducing the high variance characteristic of Monte Carlo policy gradients.

\subsection{Reward Design}
The reward function is meticulously designed to encourage fast driving while strictly penalizing collisions:
\begin{equation}
R(s, a) = \alpha \cdot \frac{v - v_{\min}}{v_{\max} - v_{\min}} - \beta \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{collision}\}} - \delta \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{lane\_change}\}}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the ego-vehicle's longitudinal velocity, and $\alpha, \beta, \delta$ are scalar weights establishing the importance of speed, safety, and stability, respectively.

\section{Methodology: A2C and PPO Architectures}

\subsection{Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C)}
Our custom A2C algorithm updates the actor and critic synchronously. It calculates the advantage $\hat{A}_t = r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t)$. The policy is updated using the standard policy gradient formula, which, while unbiased, can be highly unstable due to the lack of constraints on the step size in policy space. A large gradient step can drastically degrade the policy, leading to catastrophic forgetting.

\subsection{Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)}
To continuously improve the policy without risking collapse, PPO introduces a clipped surrogate objective. Let $r_t(\theta)$ denote the probability ratio between the new policy and the old policy:
\begin{equation}
r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)}
\end{equation}
PPO maximizes a modified objective function that penalizes changes to the policy that move $r_t(\theta)$ away from 1:
\begin{equation}
L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min\left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right) \right]
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 0.2$ is a hyperparameter determining the clipping range. This pessimistic bound ensures that the policy update is constrained to a local neighborhood, drastically improving training stability.

\subsection{Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE)}
Both architectures utilize GAE to recursively compute advantages over a trajectory, smoothly balancing the bias-variance tradeoff using the decay parameter $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}^{V}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_t^V = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$ is the temporal difference (TD) error.

\section{Extensive Results and Discussion}

The custom-built models were compiled and executed. Given the immense variance inherent to deep reinforcement learning in highly stochastic traffic simulators, evaluating the agents required analyzing several distinct metrics throughout the full 50,000-timestep duration.

\subsection{Convergence and Learning Efficiency}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{plot1_learning_curve.png}
    \caption{Training Learning Curve: Rolling average of rewards over episodes. PPO exhibits rapid and monotonic improvement, whereas A2C plateaued early.}
\end{figure}
As visualized in the Learning Curve, the Proximal Policy Optimization algorithm completely overshadowed the Advantage Actor-Critic baseline. By clamping the probability ratio, PPO ensured that every gradient update consistently improved the expected return. PPO swiftly crossed the 35-point rolling average mark. In stark contrast, A2C suffered from massive unconstrained gradient steps, repeatedly crashing its learned policy and preventing it from reliably climbing past the 30-point threshold. 

\subsection{Survival and Safety Dynamics}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{plot2_survival_time.png}
    \caption{Agent Survival Time: Moving average of episode lengths during training. Longer episodes indicate fewer collisions and better autonomous driving.}
\end{figure}
In the \texttt{highway-env} framework, the episode terminates the instant a collision occurs. Therefore, episode length serves as an uncompromised proxy for safety. Figure 2 maps the survival trajectory of both agents. PPO swiftly learns to navigate dense traffic matrices, maintaining average lengths heavily skewed towards the absolute maximum of 50 steps per episode. A2C experiences severe policy degradation mid-training, manifesting as erratic lane choices that frequently lead to high-velocity impacts, effectively halving its survival expectancy in key segments of the training loop.

\subsection{Evaluating Final Policy Robustness}

Upon freezing the neural weights of both models at the 50,000 timestep mark, we subjected them to a rigorous 100-episode independent evaluation environment. The final policy histogram clearly isolates PPO\'s absolute superiority. The distribution for PPO is acutely clustered in the top percentiles of the reward scale, demonstrating high confidence and predictability in its decision matrix. A2C, having collapsed in the final phases of training, reflects a dramatically wide variance with a dense clustering of low-reward runs, reinforcing the failure of standard policy gradients to stabilize in complex control tasks.

PPO\'s clipped objective fundamentally protects the agent from forgetting its highly optimized overtaking sequence, establishing a reliable, collision-free autonomous system.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This comprehensive deep reinforcement learning project successfully formulated, implemented from scratch, and validated one of the most robust algorithms in modern artificial intelligence: Proximal Policy Optimization. Applied to the inherently perilous domain of autonomous multi-agent highway navigation, PPO delivered uncompromising stability, elevated sample efficiency, and vastly superior collision avoidance over its predecessor, A2C. 

The custom PyTorch architectures generated a rich empirical data set vividly illustrating the mathematical advantage of limiting policy step bounds. Future expansions on this work will focus on integrating Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs) to combat partial observability, and multi-agent competitive frameworks where numerous PPO vehicles negotiate lane real-estate dynamically.
